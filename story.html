The romanization problem — why your map is lying to you

Japanese restaurant names aren't text. They're cultural artifacts: classical Chinese readings, irregular place-name pronunciations, katakana loan-words that must be back-translated to English, historical terms that predate modern Japanese. Standard romanization libraries treat them as text — and the results aren't subtle errors. They're completely different words.

This isn't transliteration. It's cultural intelligence at inference speed.

We didn't trust one AI. We ran two.

We evaluated 53,502 restaurant records independently with Claude Sonnet 5 and Claude Opus 4.8 — same dataset, same prompt, zero collaboration — then compared every verdict (53,502 total records evaluated; 51,967 shared between both models — the remainder were ASCII names or override-excluded records).

Where the data comes from

Coordinates — the pin has to be right

Clean names aren't enough; a pin on the wrong block breaks the experience. Every one of the 62,173 records carries a latitude & longitude — geocoded from its address, not inherited from a scrape.

We actively purge bad coordinates. When we found 131 overseas branches a Japan-only geocoder had silently parked on garbage domestic coordinates (a Hong Kong shop sitting in Niigata), we detected and removed every one. Each shop is then enriched with its nearest station — name, English name, and distance in metres.
